Introduction
Employment among autistic individuals is an area of noted difficulty with an employment rate well below the general population.

[1]
According to the Drexel Institute National Autism Indicators Report, only 58% of autistic young adults in the United States ever worked in their early 20s.
By comparison, 99% of young adults with no disabilities worked in their early 20s, and 90% of young adults with other disabilities - emotional disturbance, speech impairment, or other learning impairment - worked in their early 20s

[1]
Barriers attributed to autistic
unemployment can include:

  • Difficulties with communication and social interaction
  • Trouble organizing and completing workplace tasks
  • Obsessive adherence to routine


[2]
Several studies have concluded that long-term support is necessary to acquire and maintain autistic employment.

[3]
These long term supports can include access to social workers and job coaches, however these can be costly and time consuming options and may not be sustainable indefinitely.

[4]
The goal of this research is to develop a workplace e-learning style web application designed to provide long-term support for autistic employees and their managers.

User Overview
Anticipated Employee Goals
Employment is an important part of life and gives all people, regardless of disability, senses of purpose, self worth, and independence

[5]
An anticipated user goal of employees is to complete workplace tasks and be successful in their role, and having assistance available in order to do so.
A further anticipated user goal is the need feel included and have any differences be embraced.
A survey conducted by Scott et al. concluded that the majority of autistic employees surveyed felt workplace success was dependent on continued and demonstrated employer commitment [10].
Anticipated Manager Goals
Enabling the prospective autistic employee to succeed but also enhancing general business
practices such as increased:

  • Workplace learning
  • Communication
  • Job skill documentation
Further manager goals may also include maintaining a neurologically diverse workforce, which has been shown to increase innovative practices in the workplace.
The term Neurodiversity can be defined as those that have a different way of thinking, and promoting their inclusion can bring different ideas and perspectives to a workplace [11].

Communication Differences

Communication differences and difficulties are a wide ranging facet of autism.

Different individuals have different communication challenges and these differences are amplified by the general complexities of communication.

[19]
Autistic individuals can have challenges with communication in social contexts, such as:

  • Appropriate use of language in social situations.
  • Correct use of grammar.
  • The tempo and rhythm of speech. (Prosody?)
Further areas of communication difficulty can include:

  • Contextual situations and the literal meanings of words.
  • Understanding different points of view
  • Facial expressions, gestures, non verbal communication
These challenges can have noted effects on autistic employees in the workplace. A failure to understand what a manager or colleague is trying to communicate can lead to misunderstanding instructions and directions, especially when they are given verbally.

[21]—[23]
Social anxiety is another potential area of difficulty. It can be defined as “persistent fear about social situations and being around people” which can inhibit developing a comfort level with colleagues and superiors.

Application Functionality
Several pieces of application functionality were designed to act as interventions for specific neurological differences that have the potential to cause difficulties in the workplace for
autistic employees.
Interventions in the context of autism research are defined as “doing something, taking action or using a treatment to try to improve a particular condition or problem”.

[8]

Tasks and Task Steps

  • Allows managers of autistic employees to create task and subtask step cards designed to assist employees progress through workplace activities.
  • Conceived as an intervention to assist with possible executive function disorder by breaking complex workplace tasks into smaller, more manageable pieces, thus reducing cognitive load [27][37].
  • By following the task steps, the user will be assisted in progressing through a workplace activity one small step at a time [18], [36].
A further design goal was to accommodate potential challenges with verbal communication and directions

[23].
Tasks and task steps function as a nonverbal and explicit set of directions, given electronically, that the users can view multiple times if they become lost, confused, or forgetful

[38].
They also provide the user with visual aides, giving managers the ability to attach images and video for each task step to assist with visual learning.

[39]

Jargons

  • Designed as an intervention for potential communication difficulties with workplace context/concepts.
  • Give context, information, and visual support to workplace terms and ideas.
  • Developed to increase independence, help to limit potentially stressful verbal communication.
  • Allows users to search and learn workplace terms on their own.

Managers have the ability to

  • Create a Jargon for a workplace term or concept
  • Include an image to add additional visual reinforcement
  • Incorporate that Jargon into a task step. That included term is then highlighted and gains a hoverable popover box containing the chosen image and additional context.

Jots

  • Designed to act as an intervention for potential difficulties with communication between a manager and an employee.
  • A streamlined way to ask their manager questions and keep track of answers/context specific to tasks.
  • Use of emojis further convey how they are feelings about a task, and keep it light/informal, ok to be wrong.
User Interface Design
The user interface design of the application was guided by available research on the topic of effective guidelines when designing for autistic users, combined with how these guidelines could be adapted to meet the prospective goals of Wayfinder application users.
Icons, Colors, and General User Interface Choices:
Throughout the application neutral color palettes were chosen to accommodate color sensitive users.

[40], [39]
Simple icons and images were implemented across the application to reinforce the concepts and actions that they represented.

[40]
The interface was also designed to minimize the amount of content on the screen at any given time in order to minimize cognitive burden. [40]
The application’s navigation was designed to be as simple and consistent as possible, with each page clearly conveying to the user where they currently are in the application.
File System UI

The primary user interface was modeled after the file systems present in modern operating systems. The design was intended to be familiar to users and simple to navigate.

[42], [43]
Additionally, the folder directory functionality has the ability to perform a weighted search for any of the mentioned functionality. This is also intended to be a familiar style of interface, as most web users rely on searches to navigate.
The application name of Wayfinder, the application icon, and the writing tone used throughout the application, were chosen with the intention of expressing a sense of inclusion and acceptance.

Inspired by Polynesian wayfinding explorers, the hope is that the Wayfinder application conveys to the user that it is here to help you find the way.

[44], [45]
Conclusion
The primary research goal of the Wayfinder project was to illuminate how software design and user experience design can take autistic differences into consideration and provide possible interventions.
This project also highlights some of the higher goals in the use of such technology, such as active inclusion, promoting diversity, and tools for continued support for autistic adults.
The intention is that tools such as these would be a cost-effective path of least resistance, leading to workplace buy-in and, hopefully, increased autistic hiring and long-term retention.
